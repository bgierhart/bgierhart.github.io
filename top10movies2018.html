HONORABLE MENTIONS

Swimming by Mac Miller

Grammy Nominee for Best Rap Album

It's an utter shame when someone young passes away. To be honest, I hadn't heard of Mac Miller before his passing. This album is largely hit or miss, but songs like "Self Care" seemed to indicate there were great things to come. R.I.P.

Little Dark Age by MGMT

This was simply a pleasant listen. No reinventing the wheel here, but "Me and Michael" proves that not everything has to.

F.A.M.E. by Maluma

Maluma is my Latin pop star guilty pleasure. He's got a sultry voice and some great videos. This album wasn't quite the statement of his arrival in mainstream pop music that I hoped it would be, but it was welcome nonetheless. Check out "El Pr√©stamo" and "Marinero."

Art of Doubt by Metric

Raise your hand if you discovered Metric from Academy Award Winner Brie Larson's *cough* superior cover of one of their songs in Scott Pilgrim? Well, I did anyway. I'm glad I became a fan because their catalogue is a lot of fun. "Now or Never Now" is a huge song and isn't afraid to admit it.

Love Is Dead by Chvrches

Out of the 70 albums on this list, this was perhaps my biggest disappointment. Chvrches is fun and brash, unapologetic in its youthful electronic dance energy, and this one seemed to take itself a little too seriously. Check out "Miracle" though. It's classic Chvrches, albeit a little more poppy.

Shawn Mendes by Shawn Mendes

Grammy Nominee for Best Pop Vocal Album

If his previous work has solidified his placement as a teenage heartthrob, this album is the beginning of his transition into something a little more serious. He's still got a ways to go, but there's a real artist somewhere in the person who helped create "Lost In Japan."

Tranquility Base Hotel & Casino by Arctic Monkeys

Grammy Nominee for Best Alternative Music Album

I love me some Arctic Monkeys, but this one was kind of a bummer. Look, it's a concept album about a hotel on the moon. No one wanted to like it more than I did. Unfortunately, this album ended up being little more than a David Bowie impression. I mean, a decent one, but an impression nonetheless. "Four Out Of Five" is where that tactic seemed to work for them best.

Expectations by Bebe Rexha

Grammy Nominee for Best New Artist

Bebe Rexha seemed to come out of nowhere for me. I'm not completely sold that she's more than a flash in the pan, but man, "I'm A Mess" is catchy and true as hell.

Golden by Kylie Minogue

This is your first of probably several reminders that this blog is written by a gay man. This album is a hot mess, and I love it anyway. I mean, why is an Australian making a country music album with a song that prominently features a Cuban reggaeton band? Why not? Step it up, Keith Urban.

Phoenix by Rita Ora

When will Rita Ora get the mainstream American audience that she deserves? Who knows, but here's another album full of bangers for you to ignore. One of my friends called her "Value Brand Rihanna," and... I admit that's hilarious. It also hurt though.

High As Hope by Florence + the Machine

I guess I'll just have to accept that I'll forever be chasing the high of Florence's first two albums for the rest of my life. Nothing here really comes close except for "Hunger," which shows that Welch's penchant for the dramatic and killer voice are very much intact.

MANIA by Fall Out Boy

Grammy Nominee for Best Rock Album

Perhaps my guiltiest of guiltiest pleasures, I happen to think the world is better for having Fall Out Boy in it. Their album "God Save Rock and Roll" is legitimately good by any standard, and while "MANIA" doesn't quite reach the same cheekiness, it has its moments.

Pray For The Wicked by Panic! At The Disco

I'm going to admit that the sole reason this album is here is for the stellar song "High Hopes." I loved Panic! at the Disco as much as anyone else in high school and listented to their albums with dedication. "High Hopes" so perfectly encapsulates what it feels like to want to make something of yourself that it never fails to pump me up. Also, it's full of little musical easter eggs from the different sounds the band has attempted over the years. It's fun, well constructed, and if you don't like it, I don't like you.

Mean Girls Original Cast Recording by Various Artists

Tony Nominee for Best New Musical

I'm not going to waste time rolling my eyes at people who think it's lame that there are so many musicals based off of movies these days... Musicals have always been based off of the popular media of the day. It happens to be movies now, and none are more fetch than the cult-hit Mean Girls. The music and book are by Tina Fey and her husband who composed the music for 30 Rock and Unbreakable Kimmy Schmidt. It's really a slam dunk, and "Sexy" is one of several songs that never fails to make me laugh.

Lost & Found by Jorja Smith

Grammy Nominee for Best New Artist

She's one of a million young women with great voices and nice songwriting chops, but she's the standout from last year. I really hope she makes it. There's a tenderness to her that I like.

Electric Light by James Bay

"Let It Go" ‚Äì not THAT "Let It Go" ‚Äì was a massive hit for James Bay in 2014, and then he dropped off the face of the Earth. His latest seems to indicate that he spent the time in some sort of industry focus group, which is a real shame. His second album is all over the place, but it's still enjoyable. I just wish it were a little more authentic to the James Bay who appeared on his last album.

Scorpion by Drake

Grammy Nominee for Album of the Year

I have to admit something: I'm not really that big of a Drake fan. He's just a little... basic for me. That being said, I love me some "Hotline Bling," so who am I to judge? I tend to enjoy him best when he's a little goofy, and this double album is decidedly NOT that. However, I have to admit that "God's Plan" is brilliant and cements his placement in the rap pantheon.

Desperate Man by Eric Church

My mom loves Eric Church, so it is difficult for me to admit that I liked this album too. "Some Of It" is real country music and not that garbage on country radio.

To The Sunset by Amanda Shires

This is one of those albums that I never would have discovered if it weren't my obsession with keeping up with reviews on Pitchfork and Rolling Stone. It didn't quite warrant a ranking on the top 50 list, but I was really glad I listened to it.

Ordinary Corrupt Human Love by Deafheaven

I don't listen to a lot of metal music, but throw in a few melodic and operatic touches, and I'm there. "Night People" is gorgeous. True story.

50. Tha Carter V by Lil Wayne

I get that it's not cool to like Lil Wayne, and this album is definitely a fall from the days of Tha Carter III. But... I still liked it. A lot. Fight me.

49. Tell Me How You Really Feel by Courtney Barnett

There's something raw about Courtney Barnett's songwriting for me. Not in a good way, like each song is a first draft and not quite a finished product. That aspect does have its charms, however, and there are times where I welcome the unpolished quality of her music. "Need A Little Time" is the epitome of that spirit on this record.

48. COASTCITY by COASTCITY

Grammy Nominee for Best Latin Rock, Urban or Alternative Album

These two Puetro Rican musicians are pretty much nobodies who sprang to stardom simply by making some great music. It's the kind of story you don't hear too much of anymore, and I'm really happy for them. Their Grammy nomination is well deserved. That bridge in "Noches y Ma√±anas." Everything.

This isn't quite the follow-up to "We're All Gonna Die" that I hoped it would be. It seems they tried to speak perhaps a little too hard to the times we're in socio-politically speaking. 45. Girl Going Nowhere by Ashley McBride

Grammy Nominee for Best Country Album

This album epitomizes that moment when a completely unassuming person goes up to karaoke and blows you away. I had no idea I would hear one of the best country music albums of the year from a complete nobody. A mention on Obama's annual list of favorite songs of the year doesn't hurt either. Good for her.

44. Chris by Christine and the Queens

I didn't know much about Christine and the Queens before my annual music listen-through, but I'm glad I'm brought up to speed now. I can see the influence they've had on some of my favorite groups, most notably HAIM. To me anyway. Anyway, give the French side of the double album a listen if that's your thing.

43. Jake Shears by Jake Shears

Scissors Sisters fans rejoice! Jake Shears finally released a solo album, and he recorded a decent chunk of it in my hometown. I even got to hear some of this material for the first time at a special impromptu concert he threw together as a thank you to Louisville for hosting him. His energy is infections, and the album does little to lose that.

42. Sincera by Claudia Brant

Grammy Nominee for Best Latin Pop Album

Argentinian Claudia Brant is the queen of Latin jazz, and this album is a great case as for why. Her voice is pristine, and her compositions are languid. They're truly a showcase what she can do with her voice. I'm a fan for life.

41. Negro Swan by Blood Orange

Blood Orange sure knows how to produce a song to mask the pain. "Charcoal Baby" is case and point and serves as the creative zenith to "Negro Swan."

40. Vibras by J Balvin

J Balvin had quite the year. He finally broke through into the mainstream and even has a Grammy nomination as a producer for one of Cardi B's songs. I can't wait to see where the future takes him.

39. Caution by Mariah Carey

Mimi's career has had more ups and downs than a... well, Mariah Carey song, but it's nice to see her on the upswing again. "Giving Me Life" wins the award for the most appropriately titled song of the year.

38. Liberation by Christina Aguilera

Christina Aguilera's highly anticipated album seemed all but doomed to fail after fans around the world uttered a collective "Girl..." upon hearing "Accelerate" for the first time. Turns out those fears were for not, and Xtina did not disappoint. Hell, I even kind of like "Accelerate" now. The chorus anyway.

37. A Brief Inquiry Into Online Relationships by The 1975

When I first heard of this band in 2016, I really didn't think I'd like them. They seemed to me full of pretense and hollow musicianship. Thankfully, that seems to be all part of their schtick. They surprise me each time I listen to them, and I hear something new in their deceptively simple music.

36. Bloom by Troye Sivan

Nothing prepared me for when I saw the video to "Bloom" for the first time. It is unabashed and proud queerness at its finest and queerest, and I was 100 percent there for it. There's room for growth, but "Bloom" is a bold statement from an artist I cannot wait to hear more from. The future looks bright.

35. Veteran by JPEGMAFIA

There's something aggressive about JPEGMAFIA's music. It's arresting and interesting to listen to. I was completely blown away by this album, and it takes a lot for me to be impressed by rap music these days.

34. Isolation by Kali Uchis

This Colombian-American singer-songwriter sure knows how to make beat that grabs my attention. As soon as I heard "Dead To Me" for the first time, I knew I was hooked.

33. Future Me Hates Me by The Beths

The Beths remind me of a modernized version of The Shangri-Las. They're singing to that same basic heartache one feels in their teenage years when everything is so dramatic and the threat of unending loneliness seems utterly imminent. How ironic that that sentiment should make this album such a joy to listen to.

32. Lush by Snail Mail

The person behind the vocals and songwriting on display here is so prodigiously and rawly talented that it's almost too much to take in sometimes. i.e. Is this another amazing song? Or is this just average... for her. It's hard to tell sometimes. I look forward to listening to more Snail Mail and attempting to find out.

31. Interstate Gospel by Pistol Annies

Pistol Annies until now has been little more than The Miranda Lambert Band to me. There's certainly still plenty of Lambert's star power on display here, but for the first time, it appears as though they are their own independent and cohesive unit. Songs like "Milkman" really reflect on what contemporary Southern life is like now: a culture that is hellbent on judging others simply to hide the fact that they are so close to commiting sins of their own.

"Crayons" is the real highlight of this album. It's a surefire winner for the LGBTQ+ cause, and it just makes me want to move.

29. Sweetener by Ariana Grande

Grammy Nominee for Best Pop Vocal Album

Ariana Grande is a boss. If you disagree, you're just wrong. Plain and simple. Not only is her voice ridiculous, but she has the emotional fortitude to withstand some of the worst tragedies that can happen to a person, all while seemingly remaining as sweet as can be and producing mega-hit after mega-hit just to make us happy. We don't deserve her.

28. CARE FOR ME by Saba

CARE FOR ME is visceral and emotional. It's everything that good art should be. It's tough to pick one song becuase the album is such a mosaic, but if I had to choose one, I pick "LIFE." Give the whole album a try.

27. Singularity by Jon Hopkins

Grammy Nominee for Best Dance/Electronic Album

I always struggle with instrumental albums. My musical background is so heavily entrenched in vocal music that it's difficult for me to find my footing in that foreign landscape. "Singularity" hooked me though. It is absolutely enrapturing and absolutely worth your time.

26. Freedom by Amen Dunes

Amen Dunes is the quintessential Pitchfork rocker to me. Esoteric and moody. Talented and conceited but also paradoxically affable. A little pretentious but also completely indifferent to others' responses to his music. If any of that makes sense and appeals to you, you'll want to give "Freedom" a try.

25. DAYTONA by Pusha T

Grammy Nominee for Best Rap Album

Short and sweet. This album is lean and mean and full of all the punchy verse you'd expect from a rap god.

24. Look Now by Elvis Costello

I've always liked Elvis Costello, and with the help of The Imposters on this epic double album, it's nice to know that he's still cool.

23. ASTROWORLD by Travis Scott

Grammy Nominee for Best Rap Album

Epic does not even begin to describe this album. The production on this album is slick, and the narrative behind it is moving. This is easily the best thing I've ever heard from Travis Scott.

22. Gumbo Unplugged (Live) by PJ Morton

Grammy Nominee for Best R&B Album

This album breaks a lot of rules for me. I normally don't get into live albums. I have to be a super fan for that. And when I do, I always listen to studio material first. In the case of PJ Morton, I knew nothing about him until I was doing my Grammy prep and listened to this live album verison of 2017's "Gumbo." To put it simply: I was impressed. If you sound this good live, you're the shit. End of story.

21. 12 Little Spells by Esperanza Spalding

I'll never forget when Esperanza Spalding won Best New Artist at the Grammys and beat out Drake, Florence + the Machine and Justin Bieber ‚Äì wasn't sad about that last one. Who was this girl? One of the most amazing musicians of the century and a verifiable genius. That's who. This album isn't one of her best, but even her mediocre material warrants a placement this high on my list.

12. Musas (Un Homenaje Al Folclore Latinoamericano En Manos De Los Macorinos), Vol. 2 by Natalia Lafourcade

Maybe it's her ascent into a slighly more mainstream position thanks to her inclusion in the studio version of Disney's Coco's "Remember Me," but her version of her native Mexico's "La Llorona" is a tribute worthy of the gods.

11. Dirty Computer by Janelle Monae

Grammy Nominee for Album of the Year

I can't talk about this album without mentioning how awesome so many of the songs are. "Django Jane," "Pynk," and "Make Me Feel" are almost certainly the songs that will be remembered from this album and deservedly so. I want to highlight the power behind my favorite track, "So Afraid" though. Where she goes big on those other songs, she goes small here, and it works to give the album some dynamics. Love.
